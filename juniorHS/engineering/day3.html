Understanding True Stress and True Strain https://youtu.be/AkX6JqlWRqc

  1. Stress is defined as force over the initial cross-sectional area of the test specimen, and strain is defined as the change in specimen length divided by the initial length, but both are just approximations.
  2. These approximations are called engineering stress and engineering strain, and are denoted by E
  3. To determine the true values, it must be be taken into account that the dimensions of specimen change throughout the duration of the test.
  4. A true curve always increases but in the engineering curve it decreases after necking, the rapid reduction in the cross-sectional area of the test piece
  5. Necking begins when the engineering curve reaches its maximum value, which it the ultimate tensile strength of the material
  6. Two reasons for engineering over true:
    1. difficult to measure instantaneous cross-sectional area. So it's hard to make them curves
    2. Often analyzing deformation in the elastic region, where the curves have similar values
  7. The true curve is used in manufacturing processes and finite element analysis.
  8. By making a few assumptions we can calculate the true stress-strain curve based on the engineering curve and avoid instantaneous cross-sectional
  9. True stress is calculated by dividing the instantaneous cross-sectional area at each instant throughout the test. It accounts for the fact that the cross-sectional area of the test piece is changing as the test is active.
  10. We can assume constant volume because: As such Area * length is equal to the product of the original area and original length -> only valid before necking
    1. In the elastic region any volume changes in the elastic region will be small
    2. Materials are considered to be incompressible during plastic deformation
  11. True stress can be calculated by engineering stress time (1 + engineering strain)
  12. True strain can be calculated by ln (1 + engineering strain) also known as logarithmic strain or natural strain
