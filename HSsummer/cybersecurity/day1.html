Terms

CIA -> Confidentially, Integrity, Availability

Confidentially

Avoidance of the unauthorized disclosure of information. Protection of data and only allow those who need it - others are blocked.

  • Only students can check their own grades. Teachers can do so

Tools for Confidentially

  • Encryption: the transformation of information using a secret called an encryption key, so that the transformed information can only be read using another secret, called the decryption key.
    • Hackers usually "hack" during the communication channel.
    • Encrypt -> encode file
    • decrypt -> decode file using key.
    • Cipher -> means encrypted
  • Access control: Rules and policies that limit access to confidential information to those people and/or systems with a "need to know":
    • Determined by identity and which role they play.
    • Linux groups (i.e. wheel group)
  • Authentication: the determination of the identity or role that someone has.
    • Can be determined using a feature a person has like smart card, password or fingerprint.
  • Authorization: the determination if a person or system is allowed access to resources, based on an access control. Prevent attackers from tricking the system to have access to files.
  • Physical security: the establishment of physical barriers to limit access to protected computation resources:
    • Lock, Cabinets, walls with copper meshes (Faraday Cages).
    • Keyboard has a wire with electromagnetic signal -> could be read.

Integrity

The property that information has not be altered in an unauthorized way.

Tools

  • Backups: the periodic archiving of data.
  • Checksums: the computation of a function that maps the contents of a file to a numerical value. A checksum function depends on the entire contents of a file and is designed in a way that even a small change to the input file will result in a different output value.
  • Data correcting codes: methods for storing data in such a way that small changes be easily detected and automatically corrected.

Availability

The property that information is accessible and modifiable in a timely fashion by those authorized to do so.

Tools

  • Physical protections infarct suture meant to keep information available even with physical challenges.
  • Computation redundancies: computers and storage devices that serve as fullbacks in the case of failures.

Other security concepts

A.A.A -> Assurance, Authenticity, and Anonymity

Assurance

Refers to how trust is provided and am ganged in computer systems.

  • Trust management depends on:
    • Policies -> specify behavioral expectation that people or systems have for themselves and others.
    • Permissions, which describe the behaviors that are allowed by the agents that interacts with a person or system.
    • Protections: Describe mechanisms put in place to enforce permissions and policies.

Authenticity

The ability to determine that statements, policies, and permissions issued by person or systems are genuine.

  • Digital signatures: Cryptographic computations that allow a person or system to commit to the authenticity of their documents in a unique way that achieves nonrepudiation, which is the property that authentic statements issued by some person or system cannot be denied.

Anonymity

The property that certain record or transaction not to be attributable it any individual.

Tools

  • Aggregation: the combining of data from many individual so that disclosed sums or averages cannot be tied to any individual.
  • Mixing, the interlining go transactions, information, or communications in a way that cannot be traced to any individual.
  • Proxies: trusted agents that are willing to engage in actions for individual in a way that cannot be traced back to that person.
  • Pseudonyms: fictional identities that can fill in for real identities in communications and transaction, but are otherwise known only to a trusted entity.

Threats and attacks

  • Eavesdropping: the interception of information intended for someone else during its transmission over a communication channel.
  • Alteration: unauthorized modification of information:
    • The man-in-the-middle attack, when a person changes data at communication channel.
  • Denial-of-service: the interruption or degradation of a data service or information access:
    • email Spam, to the degree that is meant to simply fill up storage.
  • Masquerading: the fabrication of information that is purported to bes from someone who is not actually the author.
  • Repudiation: the denial of a commitment of data receipt:
    • Attempt to back out of a contract or a protocol that requires the different parties to provide receipts acknowledging that data has been received.
  • Correlation and traceback: the integration of multiple data sources and information flows to determine the source of a particular data stream or piece of information.
